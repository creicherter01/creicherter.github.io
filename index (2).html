Case Study 01

Find Your
Perfect Flight

I reimagined the flight booking experience to be seamless, intuitive, and delightful — a unified interface that searches hundreds of airlines and travel sites simultaneously.

View interactive prototype ↗Read case study
The Challenge

The Problem

When I started this project, I observed a critical gap in the flight booking market. Travelers were forced to navigate a fragmented, frustrating experience across multiple platforms, each with its own design language and user flow.



These issues compounded to create a broken experience where users had to search multiple websites, manually compare prices, and piece together information to make a booking decision. I knew there had to be a better way.

1
Confusing Websites

Booking platforms overwhelm users with cluttered interfaces, unclear information hierarchies, and inconsistent design patterns.

2
Difficult Navigation

The booking process requires jumping between multiple steps and screens. Users lose context and must repeat information entry.

3
Excessive Unnecessary Information

Too many options, irrelevant details, and competing CTAs distract from the core task. Users experience decision fatigue.

Discovery to Solution

Pain Points & Design Iterations

Through user research, I identified specific pain points and designed targeted solutions to address each one.

1
Pain Point
Confusion Between Departure & Return Flights — Users frequently didn't know which leg they were booking, leading to mistakes and backtracking.
Design Solution
Clear visual separation with prominent headers, distinct colour coding, and a progress indicator showing which leg of the journey they're booking.
2
Pain Point
Unclear Confirmation Method — Users were often unsure whether booking would be confirmed via email or text, causing anxiety about their reservation.
Design Solution
A clear confirmation screen that explicitly states how the booking is confirmed and outlines next steps — removing all ambiguity.
3
Pain Point
Poor Flight Details Highlighting — Duration, number of stops, and arrival time were buried or easy to miss in the original interface.
Design Solution
Redesigned flight cards with clear visual hierarchy, larger typography, strategic colour use, and icons for key details at the top of each card.
4
Pain Point
Unclear Baggage Information — Users struggled to understand allowances, fees, and what was included with their ticket.
Design Solution
A simplified baggage page with clear sections for included baggage, additional options, and pricing with visual icons and plain language.
5
Pain Point
Third-Party Seat Selection — Redirecting to a third-party seat picker broke the seamless booking flow and created friction.
Design Solution
Seat selection integrated directly into the booking flow with an interactive seat map — no leaving the platform.
6
Shared Value
Efficiency — Every participant valued their time and wanted a streamlined experience above all else.
Design Solution
The entire flow designed around efficiency — fewer form fields, no unnecessary steps, a clear linear path from search to confirmation.
My Approach

Design Process

I followed a rigorous, user-centred design process to understand the problem deeply and create a solution that truly resonates with travellers.

Step 1
User Research & Testing

In-depth usability testing with frequent flight bookers to understand pain points, behaviours, and needs.

  • Interviewed 12+ users across demographics
  • Observed real booking sessions
  • Identified common frustrations and needs
Step 2
Synthesis & Analysis

Organised research findings into affinity diagrams and detailed journey maps for each booking stage.

  • Built affinity diagrams from research notes
  • Mapped 5 key journey stages with emotions
  • Identified critical moments and opportunities
Step 3
Sketches & Ideation

Sketched multiple approaches across different information architectures and interaction patterns.

  • Rapid sketching of multiple solutions
  • Explored different information hierarchies
  • Selected most promising directions
Step 4
Prototyping & Refinement

Built medium-fidelity prototypes for testing, then high-fidelity with a complete visual design system.

  • Built medium-fidelity prototypes for testing
  • Iterated based on user feedback
  • Created final high-fidelity design system

A unified flight search interface that simplifies the entire booking journey. Users compare prices, airlines, and times in one beautiful, intuitive space.

Case Study 02

Goodreads
Redesign

Modernising a beloved but neglected reading platform — preserving the cozy, bookish warmth while fixing a decade of design debt and rethinking the flows readers rely on every day.

View interactive prototype ↗ Read case study
The Challenge

The Problem

Goodreads has over 150 million users and the largest book review database on the internet — yet its design hasn't meaningfully evolved since Amazon acquired it in 2013.



The platform retains its loyal user base through network effects and utility alone, while competitors like StoryGraph and Literal win over users who want the same functionality in a product that feels cared for.



This redesign honours the warmth Goodreads is known for while fixing the structural problems that make it frustrating to use every day.

1
Noisy, Low-Signal Home Feed

Ads, generic recommendations, and friend updates are mixed with no way to filter or prioritise the content users actually come for.

2
Painful Progress Logging

Updating reading progress requires 3+ taps with no persistent shortcut — a high-friction interaction for the most common daily action.

3
Weak Discovery Despite Rich Data

After 15+ years of shelf data, recommendations feel generic. Community lists are buried despite being the best discovery surface.

4
Visual Design Frozen in 2011

Inconsistent components, a cramped layout, and no evident design system make the product feel unmaintained and hard to trust.

User Research

Research & Key Insights

I combined desk research, competitive audit, user interviews, and usability walkthroughs to build a complete picture of where Goodreads fails its users.

01
Research Method
Desk Research — Analysed 200+ App Store reviews, Reddit threads on r/goodreads and r/books, and public UX teardowns to identify recurring complaint themes.
Key Finding
4 of 5 interviewees described Goodreads as a habit they tolerate, not a product they love. "I wouldn't recommend it" appeared repeatedly.
02
Research Method
Competitive Audit — Side-by-side evaluation of StoryGraph, Literal, and Letterboxd across 8 core tasks including finding a book, logging progress, and writing a review.
Key Finding
Letterboxd proves social media for media tracking can be beautifully designed. Goodreads' closest analogue is doing it better in every design dimension.
03
Research Method
User Interviews — 5 moderated sessions (45 min each) with active Goodreads users across reading frequency levels.
Key Finding
Logging reading progress was the most common daily action and universally described as "more steps than it should be." The cozy feel was mentioned unprompted as something to preserve.
04
Research Method
Usability Walkthrough — Task-based walkthroughs of 6 core journeys on current Goodreads (web + mobile), noting friction points, dead ends, and confusion.
Key Finding
Community lists were the most loved discovery feature. Algorithmic recommendations were largely ignored — users preferred human-curated lists over algorithmic suggestions.
User Personas

Who We're Designing For

Three distinct user archetypes emerged from interviews, each with a different primary motivation for using Goodreads.

SR
Persona 01
The Habit Tracker

28, grad student. Reads 30+ books/year. Uses Goodreads daily to log progress and hit her reading challenge goal.

  • Needs quick progress logging
  • Wants prominent reading stats
  • Values the annual challenge tracker
MK
Persona 02
The Social Reader

41, teacher. Reads ~12 books/year. Comes to see what friends are reading and leave thoughtful reviews.

  • Needs a clean friend activity feed
  • Wants richer review tools
  • Values discussion boards
LP
Persona 03
The Discoverer

34, designer. Reads ~8 books/year. Comes to Goodreads for community lists and genre rabbit holes.

  • Needs curated lists front and centre
  • Wants mood-based filtering
  • Values genre deep-dives

The Result

Design Decisions

Each design decision maps directly back to a research finding. The redesign preserves the warmth of Goodreads while resolving its most critical usability failures.

Friend activity surfaces first — no more noise from ads
Feed filter pills let users control what they see in one tap
Progress update widget is persistent — one click from anywhere
Reading stats front and centre on the shelves page
Edition notice surfaces bad-data problem with a one-click fix
Community lists elevated to first-class discovery surface
Mood-based filtering replaces the weak genre taxonomy
Warm cream palette and serif type preserve the cozy brand feel
